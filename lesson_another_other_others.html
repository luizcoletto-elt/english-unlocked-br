Two Ways of Making Friends

There are many ways to make friends, but most people use one of two approaches. One way is to be very open and talkative from the start. The other is to be quieter, letting the relationship develop gradually over time. Both styles have their advantages.

People who use the first approach often start another conversation before the first one ends. They quickly talk about one topic after the other, which can feel energetic and engaging. However, other people may find this intensity a little too much at first.

On the other hand, those who prefer the quieter style often think that listening is as important as speaking. Others say that strong friendships require patience. Either way, both groups agree on one thing: good relationships take effort, in one way or another.

Grammar Focus
Understanding another, other, and others.
All three words share the same root, but are used in different ways. The key is knowing what kind of noun, if any, follows each word.
Anotherdeterminer / pronoun — singular countable
Use another before a singular countable noun, or alone as a pronoun. It means “one more of the same kind” or “a different one.”
  • Can I have another coffee? (one more)
  • This chair is broken. Let's find another one.(a different one)
  • We'll talk about it another time.(a different occasion)
  • I didn't like this hotel. Let's try another.(pronoun, alone)
Another + uncountable noun is not possible. Avoid: “another water” or “another advice.” Restructure: “another glass of water,” “another piece of advice.”
Otherdeterminer — plural countable or uncountable
Use other before a plural countable or uncountable noun. It means “additional” or “different.”
  • Other students didn't understand either.(additional/different)
  • She speaks other languages too.(different/additional)
  • Do you have any other information?(uncountable)
  • I have two cats. The other one is sleeping.(specific second of a pair)
The other (with the) refers to a specific second item in a known pair: “I have two brothers. One lives here; the other lives in São Paulo.”
Otherspronoun only — never followed by a noun
Others is a pronoun. It replaces “other + plural noun” and is never followed by a noun.
  • Some people like jazz. Others prefer rock. (= other people)
  • I finished. Have others finished too?(= other people)
  • These shoes fit. The others are too small.(= the other shoes)
  • Some agreed; others were not so sure.(pronoun, alone)
Others + noun is always wrong: “others students,” “others places.” If a noun follows, use other: “other students,” “other places.”

Pronunciation
A sound common to all three words.
The voiced TH sound
What is it?
another /æˈnʌðər/ other /ˈʌðər/ others /ˈʌðərz/

All three words contain the sound /ð/ — a sound that makes you vibrate your vocal cords. Put the tip of your tongue between your upper and lower front teeth and let air pass through while your voice box vibrates.

Common mistake for Brazilian speakers

Portuguese does not have the /ð/ sound. Speakers often substitute it with /d/ (as in dia), producing anoder instead of another. Practice by whispering the sound first. If it comes out as /d/, your tongue is not touching your teeth enough. You should feel like a bee buzzing: touch your throat and feel it vibrating while you produce the /ð/ sound alone.

Going Deeper
Common expressions with other.
each other
used to say that two or more people do the same thing reciprocally
They looked at each other and smiled.
in other words
used to introduce a simpler or clearer explanation
Dedication is important. In other words, you need to study more.
on the other hand
used to present a contrasting point of view
It's expensive. On the other hand, the quality is excellent.
the other day
a short time ago (informal)
I saw your colleague the other day at the market.
the other side / end
the opposite or far side of a place or object

She lives on the other side of the city.

He was waiting at the other end of the platform.

some other time
used to suggest a different, unspecified occasion (often a polite decline)

"Want to grab a coffee?"

"I can't today, but let's do it some other time."
